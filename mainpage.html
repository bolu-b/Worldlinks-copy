MPLS vs SD-WAN: which is right for your Nigerian bank branches?

By Worldlinks Limited  ·  June 2025  ·  6 min read

If you manage IT infrastructure for a Nigerian bank, microfinance institution, or any organisation with multiple branches, you have probably heard both terms — MPLS and SD-WAN — and wondered which one makes more sense for your network. The honest answer is: it depends, but the factors that matter most in Nigeria are quite different from what you would read in a generic global guide.

This article breaks down both technologies clearly, compares them on the dimensions that matter most to Nigerian organisations, and helps you decide which direction to go.

What is MPLS?

Multiprotocol Label Switching (MPLS) is a private network technology where your carrier routes traffic through a dedicated, managed backbone — completely separate from the public internet. Traffic is labelled and switched at high speed, making it extremely predictable in terms of latency and reliability.

In Nigeria, MPLS is typically delivered by carriers like MainOne, Spectranet, or Glo. Your branches connect via leased lines to the carrier's Points of Presence (PoPs), and traffic travels over their private infrastructure. The bank's data never touches the public internet.

💡 Key benefit: MPLS gives you a predictable, low-latency, fully private network — which is why most Tier-1 Nigerian banks still rely on it for their core branch interconnects.

What is SD-WAN?

Software-Defined Wide Area Network (SD-WAN) is a newer approach that uses software to intelligently route traffic across multiple underlying connections — which can include broadband internet, 4G/LTE, fibre, or even MPLS circuits. A central controller monitors all links in real time and sends each traffic type over the best available path.

For example, a VoIP call might always route over the lowest-latency link, while a bulk file backup uses whichever link has the most available bandwidth at that moment. If one link fails, traffic automatically shifts to another — with zero manual intervention.

Head-to-head comparison

FactorMPLSSD-WAN
LatencyVery low and consistentDepends on underlying links
ReliabilityHigh (single managed path)Very high (automatic failover across links)
CostHigh — especially for many branchesLower — can use cheaper broadband links
SecurityInherently private — no public internetRequires encryption (IPSec/TLS tunnels)
Setup timeWeeks to months per siteDays — hardware ships pre-configured
ScalabilitySlow — new circuits take time to provisionFast — add a site by shipping a device
Nigerian coverageLimited to carrier PoP locationsWorks anywhere with internet or 4G

The Nigerian context: what changes the equation

1. Last-mile reliability is unpredictable

In many parts of Nigeria — particularly outside Lagos and Abuja — leased-line delivery is unreliable. A contracted MPLS circuit may experience frequent outages, and SLA credits don't offset the business impact of a branch being offline. SD-WAN's ability to bond multiple connections (e.g. fibre + 4G backup) often delivers better real-world uptime than a single MPLS circuit.

2. Branch sprawl is expensive with MPLS

Nigerian banks typically have 100–400+ branches. At MPLS pricing, connecting every branch on a private circuit becomes prohibitively expensive. SD-WAN lets you deploy affordable broadband at smaller branches while reserving MPLS for your highest-traffic, most critical locations like regional headquarters and data centre-connected sites.

3. Regulatory considerations

The CBN's guidelines on outsourcing and IT risk management require banks to maintain adequate controls over data in transit. Both MPLS and properly configured SD-WAN can meet these requirements — but your SD-WAN deployment must include strong encryption and centralised policy management to satisfy a CBN audit.

Our recommendation

For most Nigerian financial institutions, the answer in 2025 is a hybrid approach: retain MPLS for your core interconnects (headquarters, data centres, high-value branches) and deploy SD-WAN at all other branches. This gives you the security and predictability of MPLS where it matters most, and the flexibility and cost savings of SD-WAN everywhere else.

How solar + inverter systems can save your business during grid outages

By Worldlinks Limited  ·  May 2025  ·  5 min read

Anyone running a business in Nigeria knows the reality: the national grid is unreliable. Whether you are in Lagos, Abuja, Port Harcourt, or Kano, power outages can last hours or even days. For businesses that depend on uptime — banks, hospitals, telecoms sites, logistics hubs — this is not just an inconvenience. Every hour offline is lost revenue, damaged equipment, and frustrated customers.

The traditional solution has been diesel generators. But in 2025, solar + inverter hybrid systems have become a far smarter, more cost-effective alternative for most business sites. Here is what you need to know.

How a hybrid solar system works

A hybrid solar system combines three components:

  • Solar panels — generate DC electricity from sunlight
  • Battery bank — stores energy for use when panels aren't generating (night, cloudy days)
  • Hybrid inverter — converts DC to AC, manages charging from both solar and grid, and switches seamlessly between sources

When the grid is available, it charges your batteries and runs your load. When the grid fails, the inverter switches to battery + solar power — typically within milliseconds, so computers and network equipment don't even notice the change.

⚡ Key difference from a basic inverter: A hybrid system adds solar panels to charge the batteries, dramatically reducing how often you need to rely on the grid or a generator. Your batteries stay full even during extended outages.

Sizing a system for a network site in Lagos or Abuja

The right system size depends on your load — what equipment needs to run and for how long. Here is a typical calculation for a small-to-medium network/comms site:

EquipmentWattageHours/dayDaily usage (Wh)
Router + switches80W24h1,920 Wh
Server/NAS150W24h3,600 Wh
CCTV system60W24h1,440 Wh
Lighting (LED)100W10h1,000 Wh
Workstations ×4320W10h3,200 Wh
Total daily load~11,160 Wh

For this load, with the goal of 12 hours of backup autonomy and a solar array that recharges daily, you would typically need a 5–8 kWh lithium battery bank, a 5 kVA hybrid inverter, and 8–12 solar panels (400W each), depending on roof space and orientation.

Is your site suitable?

Most commercial sites in Nigeria are well-suited for solar. The key requirements are unshaded roof or compound space (at least 20–40 sqm), a load profile that doesn't include very heavy motor loads, and a secure storage area for the battery bank and inverter.

5 signs your telecom tower needs maintenance before it fails

By Worldlinks Limited  ·  April 2025  ·  4 min read

A telecom tower or communication mast is built to last — but only with regular maintenance. In Nigeria's climate, where heat, humidity, salt air (near the coast), and seasonal rainfall all accelerate corrosion and structural fatigue, a tower that looks fine from the ground may be quietly deteriorating in ways that put your equipment — and your people — at risk.

The good news is that most tower failures are preventable. Here are five warning signs that your tower needs attention before a minor issue becomes an expensive emergency.

Sign 1: Visible surface rust or corrosion

Steel towers are galvanised or painted to resist corrosion, but this protection degrades over time — especially at joints, bolt connections, and areas where water pools. Surface rust that looks cosmetic is often the first stage of structural corrosion that weakens the steel below.

What to look for: Orange or brown staining on members or bolts, bubbling paint, white powder deposits on galvanised sections. If you can see rust from the ground, the situation is almost certainly worse up close.

What to do: Schedule an inspection. Early-stage corrosion can be treated with rust converters and protective coatings at a fraction of the cost of replacing corroded members.

Sign 2: Loose or missing bolts and fasteners

Vibration from wind loading, equipment operation, and thermal expansion causes bolted connections to loosen over time. A single loose bolt at a critical joint can cause a cascading failure, especially during high winds.

⚠️ Nigerian towers face particularly high wind loads during harmattan and rainy season storms. Bolt torque checks should be part of every annual inspection — not just visual surveys.

What to look for: Nuts that can be turned by hand, elongated bolt holes (a sign of bolt movement under load), or cracks in the steel around bolt holes.

Sign 3: Tower lean or visible deformation

A properly constructed tower should be perfectly vertical (within defined tolerances). Any visible lean is a serious warning sign. Lean can result from foundation settlement, overloading with too many antenna arrays, or structural member failure.

How to check: Sight up the tower from the base. For guyed masts, check that all guy wires have equal tension — slack guys are a common cause of lean. A structural engineer should assess any tower with visible lean before it is climbed again.

Sign 4: Cable management issues

Feeder cables and power cables that are poorly secured or have damaged outer jackets are both a maintenance problem and a safety hazard. UV degradation cracks cable jackets, water ingress causes intermittent signal faults, and unsecured cables under wind load can damage connectors and antennas.

What to look for: Cables hanging loose from their cable tray, cracked or discoloured jacketing, missing cable clamps, and water pooling at cable entry points into equipment shelters.

Sign 5: Your last maintenance was more than 12 months ago

Even if you cannot see any of the above symptoms, a tower that has not been professionally inspected in over a year should be considered overdue. Industry best practice, and the standard Worldlinks follows for all managed tower assets, is an annual preventive maintenance visit including:

  • Full visual structural inspection, top to bottom
  • Bolt torque check on all critical connections
  • Corrosion treatment and protective coating touch-up
  • Cable management inspection and rectification
  • Grounding and earthing system check
  • Written report with photographic evidence and recommendations

The cost of waiting

Emergency tower repairs — or worse, a collapse — cost many times more than scheduled maintenance. Beyond the financial cost, a tower failure means extended site downtime, potential equipment loss, and in the worst case, injury to personnel or members of the public below.

APN / M2M Solutions

Worldlinks Limited  ·  Private Mobile Network Services

A private APN (Access Point Name) gives your organisation its own dedicated mobile data channel — completely separate from regular consumer mobile internet. Every SIM card on your APN connects only to your systems, through your firewall, on your IP range.

Key benefits

  • All device traffic routes through your private network — not the public internet
  • Fixed private IP addresses for every device (ATM, POS, router, sensor)
  • Centralised management — block, allow, or monitor any device remotely
  • Works across all major Nigerian mobile networks (MTN, Airtel, Glo, 9mobile)

Common use cases

  • ATM machines — secure, always-on connectivity with dual-SIM failover
  • POS terminals — fast transaction processing on a private channel
